\chapter{Research Objectives}\label{chap:objectives}

The primary aim of this thesis is to evaluate the capacity of Large Language Models (LLMs) to autonomously synthesize bespoke optimization heuristics that are mathematically resilient to stochastic environments. To systematically bridge the gap between existing deterministic Automated Algorithm Design (AAD) frameworks and noisy, real-world applications, this research is driven by three core objectives and their corresponding research questions:

% ==============================================================================
% OBJECTIVE 1
% ==============================================================================
\section*{Objective 1: Formulating a Stochastic Evaluation Sandbox}

To test the adaptive limits of an LLM, the execution environment must accurately reflect non-deterministic conditions by transforming deterministic functions into noisy problem spaces. Currently, standard AAD frameworks evaluate generated code exclusively against clean, theoretical baselines. This objective seeks to answer the following core question:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{RQ1: How can continuous optimization benchmarks be systematically extended to simulate stochastic landscapes?}
\end{quote}

To address this challenge, this objective centers on the engineering and implementation of a non-deterministic wrapper layer over continuous benchmark functions. Grounded in established optimization literature \cite{Jin2005, Hansen2009Noisy}, this phase focuses on evaluating how the systematic application of a statistical noise distribution deforms baseline evaluations. The primary goal is to establish a repeatable, secure evaluation harness that effectively masks exact analytical gradients and forces an automated agent to navigate corrupted fitness values, thereby creating the necessary environmental pressure to test for genuine stochastic adaptation in language model code synthesis.

% ==============================================================================
% OBJECTIVE 2
% ==============================================================================
\section*{Objective 2: Autonomous Synthesis of Noise-Resilient Heuristics}

While recent literature confirms that automated frameworks can generate highly competitive algorithms for clean, noiseless functions, it remains an open theoretical boundary whether these generative loops can adapt to stochastic disruption. Consequently, this objective addresses the core generative question of the thesis:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{RQ2: Can Large Language Models autonomously synthesize optimization heuristics that are resilient to noise?}
\end{quote}

To evaluate this capability, the generative pipeline will be deployed across a representative subset of continuous mathematical landscapes. Rather than manually hard-coding statistical interventions or filtering mechanisms for the generative model, this objective focuses on evaluating the LLM's capacity to autonomously transition from deterministic logic to noise-aware strategies solely through closed-loop empirical evaluations. The desired outcome is to establish whether the evolutionary pressures of a stochastic environment can drive an LLM to independently discover, program, and refine robust, bespoke uncertainty-handling logic within executable structural code classes.

% ==============================================================================
% OBJECTIVE 3
% ==============================================================================
\section*{Objective 3: Empirical Benchmarking and Cross-Environment Analysis}

It is critical to formally validate the performance profiles of these LLM-generated algorithms under uncertainty and isolate how their robustness shifts across varied problem conditions. This evaluation drives the final comparative question:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{RQ3: How does the cross-environment robustness of noise-evolved heuristics compare against deterministic baselines?}
\end{quote}

To validate the significance of noise-aware evolution, we will establish a rigorous comparative benchmarking framework that evaluates runtime convergence rates and solution quality within a corrupted environment. Evolved champion algorithms will be evaluated against control groups synthesized under clean conditions as well as traditional baselines. Testing these suites across identical stochastic setups allows this objective to systematically measure the exact performance differential yielded by exposing the generative model to persistent uncertainty. Finally, this phase will culminate in a comprehensive performance analysis, directly comparing our empirical results with findings from baseline literature operating under clean, noiseless criteria to isolate the structural and behavioral boundaries an automated framework independently exhibits under severe uncertainty.
