Historically, the creation of efficient continuous optimization algorithms required rigid mathematical derivations crafted by human experts over years of trial and error. The field is currently experiencing a paradigm shift towards Automated Algorithm Design (AAD), a methodology that transforms Large Language Models (LLMs) into autonomous algorithmic inventors. Recent high-profile literature has definitively proven that LLMs, when structured within a rigorous evolutionary loop, possess the semantic and mathematical reasoning necessary to discover novel programmatic solutions that can rival or surpass human-engineered methodologies \cite{Romera2023}. Instead of prompting an AI to output a static answer, modern AAD architectures construct an automated ``discovery loop.'' The system prompts the LLM to write executable Python code for a novel optimization solver, which is then executed in a secure sandbox and scored against a target empirical metric.

To operationalize this discovery loop, this research leverages the recent Large Language Model Evolutionary Algorithm (LLaMEA) framework \cite{LLaMEA2024}. The quantitative results of the sandbox evaluations---alongside any runtime errors or performance penalties---are fed directly back to the LLM via LLaMEA's architecture. This iterative feedback loop allows the AI to autonomously debug, mutate, and evolve its own mathematical logic. While AAD frameworks like LLaMEA show immense promise, their application to unpredictable, real-world stochastic environments remains a critical open challenge. This thesis directs this LLM-driven pipeline at a major industrial bottleneck: discovering physical parameters from noisy sensor data. Consider Electric Vehicle (EV) manufacturing, where engineers rely on continuous black-box optimization to extract exact physical parameters (e.g., internal electrical resistance) from battery sensor data. Real-world sensors are cheap and prone to electrical interference, producing highly stochastic data that renders the mathematical search space incredibly jagged and deceptive.

Traditional optimization heuristics, such as the widely utilized Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) \cite{Hansen2001}, are overwhelmingly designed for deterministic, clean landscapes. When applied to heavily noisy environments, these traditional algorithms frequently break down---chasing false statistical spikes and failing to converge without extensive, manually engineered statistical interventions \cite{Hansen2009}. Manually designing a robust mathematical algorithm to handle this uncertainty is notoriously labor-intensive. This thesis bypasses the human bottleneck entirely. By applying the LLaMEA framework to heavily stochastic, simulated landscapes, we task an LLM with autonomously generating, testing, and discovering the specific mathematical mechanisms (e.g., dynamic smoothing, momentum, adaptive step sizes) required to cut through real-world sensor noise.
