\chapter{Introduction and Motivation}\label{chap:intro}

Historically, the engineering of efficient continuous optimization algorithms required rigid mathematical derivations and extensive manual trial-and-error processes conducted by human experts over decades \cite{Nelder1965, Hansen2001}. Developing robust search heuristics requires navigating complex topological landscapes while preventing premature local convergence—a manual loop that represents a severe engineering bottleneck, as custom-tailoring unique algorithmic logic for every distinct industrial application is heavily labor-intensive and unscalable \cite{Burke2013}. To eliminate this human-expert limitation, the field of operations research is experiencing a profound paradigm shift towards Automated Algorithm Design (AAD) driven by Large Language Models (LLMs). Rather than acting as static text interfaces, modern foundation models are embedded within structured, closed-loop evolutionary environments capable of autonomously writing, testing, and refining complete executable source code structures that rival or surpass traditional human-engineered baselines \cite{Romera2023}.

To operationalize this autonomous code-generation loop, this research leverages the recent Large Language Model Evolutionary Algorithm (LLaMEA) framework \cite{LLaMEA2024}. The system pipeline prompts the generative model to output fully functional Python optimization classes, executes the generated script within a secure validation sandbox against standard mathematical benchmarks, and iteratively feeds raw execution metrics and compiler tracebacks directly back into the LLM's context window to drive self-directed mutation and logical debugging. While existing literature proves that frameworks like LLaMEA deliver exceptional optimization results when evaluated under complete determinism, their capability to adapt code structures to unpredictable, non-deterministic conditions remains a major unaddressed boundary layer. This thesis directly targets this research gap by transitioning the LLaMEA pipeline into continuous black-box optimization landscapes corrupted by persistent statistical noise, forcing the generative model to independently discover and implement noise-resilient mathematical constructs to survive severe uncertainty.

Traditional optimization heuristics, such as the widely utilized Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) \cite{Hansen2001}, are overwhelmingly designed under the assumption of deterministic, noiseless landscapes. When applied to heavily noisy environments, these traditional ranking-based algorithms frequently break down---chasing false statistical spikes and failing to converge without extensive, manually engineered statistical interventions \cite{Hansen2009Noisy}. Manually designing a robust mathematical algorithm to handle this uncertainty is notoriously labor-intensive, as a fundamentally poor candidate solution may temporarily yield a high fitness score purely due to a favorable statistical anomaly, causing search rules to chase false gradients. This structural frailty is thoroughly documented in classic metaheuristic reviews, which verify that stochastic noise operating within the fitness space systematically corrupts population ranking operators and destroys the path convergence properties of hand-crafted heuristics \cite{Jin2005}. This vulnerability is further magnified if one attempts to use classical gradient-based optimization algorithms, such as Quasi-Newton methods or classical Gradient Descent; because these systems rely entirely on computing exact analytical or finite-difference derivatives, the addition of even minor statistical noise completely corrupts gradient estimation vectors, leading to immediate algorithmic failure \cite{Sarafian2024}. This thesis completely bypasses the human expert bottleneck by shifting the focus from static parameter tuning to the autonomous synthesis of adaptive code structures capable of operating inside these turbulent environments.

Rather than attempting to solve a single isolated industrial engineering task or manipulating raw physical hardware data, this thesis establishes a fundamental, benchmark-driven computer science evaluation to isolate the capabilities of Automated Algorithm Design under uncertainty. We emulate the chaotic behaviors of physical hardware sensors by constructing a controlled stochastic layer directly over the continuous Black-Box Optimization Benchmark (BBOB) suite \cite{Hansen2010}. By systematically corrupting synthetic math landscapes with parameter-controlled noise distributions, we establish a clean, repeatable evaluation loop to test the adaptive limits of the LLaMEA framework \cite{LLaMEA2024}. This abstract configuration enables this research to study the core generative problem directly: assessing whether the evolutionary pressure of a noisy context loop can compel an LLM to autonomously discover and output generic, programmatic Python optimization code tailored to handle uncertainty.

Consequently, the core contribution of this thesis is divided into three distinct methodological phases designed to establish a clear indicator of language model adaptability under severe uncertainty. First, we formulate a non-deterministic evaluation sandbox by building a dynamic wrapper layer capable of embedding controlled statistical profiles onto standard benchmark problems. Second, we evaluate the generative model's capacity to modify Python-based metaheuristic class structures when exposed exclusively to these corrupted landscapes over multiple evolutionary cycles. Finally, this research maps out a rigorous cross-environment robustness test, validating our noise-trained champion models directly against equivalents evolved under clean, deterministic criteria to isolate the structural logic an AI independently invents to protect its search path from deception.

This iterative feedback loop allows the AI to autonomously debug, mutate, and evolve its own mathematical logic. While AAD frameworks like LLaMEA show immense promise, their application to unpredictable, real-world stochastic environments remains a critical open challenge. This thesis directs this LLM-driven pipeline at a major industrial bottleneck: discovering physical parameters from noisy sensor data. Consider Electric Vehicle (EV) manufacturing, where engineers rely on continuous black-box optimization to extract exact physical parameters (e.g., internal electrical resistance) from battery sensor data. Real-world sensors are cheap and prone to electrical interference, producing highly stochastic data that renders the mathematical search space incredibly jagged and deceptive.
